
## Task 1: Business Problem Statement

### Business Problem
The company has launched a new onboarding and activation campaign for a subscription-based digital product. Users were divided into two experiment groups: a Control group, which received the existing onboarding experience, and a Treatment group, which received the new campaign experience.
The key business decision is whether the new onboarding and activation campaign should be rolled out to all users or whether the existing onboarding experience should continue.
Decision to Be Made
Leadership needs to decide whether the Treatment group experience should be launched to the full user base. This decision should be based on whether the new campaign improves user conversion and early engagement compared with the existing onboarding experience.

### Who the Decision Impacts
This decision impacts:
•	New users joining the platform
•	Product and growth teams responsible for onboarding
•	Marketing teams managing activation campaigns
•	Customer success teams supporting early-stage users
•	Company revenue and subscription growth
A successful rollout could improve user activation, subscription conversion, and early product engagement. However, an unsuccessful rollout could reduce user experience quality, increase churn risk, or negatively affect business performance.

### Success Metric That Should Improve
The primary success metric should be conversion rate, such as the percentage of users who move from signup or onboarding to subscription activation.
The campaign should also improve early engagement metrics, such as:
•	Activation rate
•	Feature usage during the first few days
•	Number of sessions after signup
•	Time spent on the product
•	Completion of onboarding steps

### Risks That Must Be Monitored
Before launching the campaign to all users, the company must monitor important guardrail metrics to ensure that the new campaign does not create negative side effects.
Key risks include:
•	Increase in early churn
•	Drop in user engagement after onboarding
•	Lower product satisfaction
•	Increase in support tickets or complaints
•	Higher unsubscribe or cancellation rate
•	Poor experience for specific user segments
The treatment should not be launched if it improves conversion but harms user experience, retention, or long-term product usage.

### Evidence Required Before Recommendation
A recommendation should be made only after comparing the Control and Treatment groups using experiment data.
The required evidence includes:
•	Whether the Treatment group shows a meaningful improvement in conversion rate
•	Whether the improvement is statistically reliable and not due to random chance
•	Whether early engagement metrics improved or remained stable
•	Whether guardrail metrics did not show negative impact
•	Whether the sample size is sufficient to support the decision
•	Whether results are consistent across important user segments

If the Treatment group improves conversion and engagement without harming guardrail metrics, the campaign can be recommended for full rollout. If results are weak, mixed, or show negative side effects, the company should either continue testing, modify the campaign, or retain the existing onboarding experience.
